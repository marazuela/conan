## Flag 2: INHALE-1 Data Quality — SIGNIFICANT RED

This is the most important finding of the deep dive.

**INHALE-1 Design**:
- 26-week Phase 3, open-label, randomized trial
- N = 230 subjects ages 4-17 with type 1 or type 2 diabetes
- Arms: Afrezza (prandial) + basal insulin vs MDI (rapid-acting analog) + basal insulin
- **Primary endpoint**: non-inferior change in HbA1c over 26 weeks, NI margin = 0.4%

**Actual result**:
- **Full ITT analysis**: between-group difference 0.435% — **exceeded the 0.4% NI margin — endpoint MISSED**
- **mITT analysis (excluding one non-adherent subject)**: 0.370% — **within NI margin — endpoint met**
- Lung function: no significant differences Afrezza vs MDI (safety OK)
- Hypoglycemia: no significant differences (safety OK)
- Treatment satisfaction: higher in Afrezza arm
- Weight gain: reduced in Afrezza arm (secondary benefit)

**Regulatory implications**:
- FDA has in the past accepted post-hoc mITT analyses when they're justified by documented protocol non-compliance, but the bar is HIGH and the analysis has to be pre-specified or at least pre-planned.
- Excluding a single subject to rescue a primary endpoint from a 0.035% miss is the kind of analysis that FDA review divisions scrutinize intensely and that can absolutely result in a CRL for "insufficient evidence of efficacy."
- The secondary endpoints (treatment satisfaction, reduced weight gain, no safety concerns) provide a benefit-risk framing that FAVORS approval, but they cannot carry an sBLA where the primary efficacy endpoint is disputed.
- **No AdCom has been called**, which could cut either way:
  - Bullish interpretation: FDA is comfortable making the call without external input (similar to TVTX)
  - Bearish interpretation: FDA does not need external input to say no, because the ITT miss is a cleaner regulatory decline rationale

**Assessment**: This is a **material negative** that the Session 23 prelim did not account for. The Contemporary Pediatrics headline "Afrezza inhaled insulin meets primary endpoint in Phase 3 pediatric diabetes trial" is misleading — it refers to the mITT analysis, not the ITT.
